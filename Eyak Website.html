The Sound System of Eyak (dAXunhyuuga’)

Olivia Waring

As you begin your studies of Eyak, one of the most daunting challenges you will encounter is wrapping your mind (and mouth!) around the sound inventory, which differs in many ways from that of English. In this section, you will be equipped with the tools to describe and produce unfamiliar sounds (which linguists sometimes refer to as phonemes).

A Crash Course in Phonetics

Linguists who study the physiological mechanisms by which sounds are produced are called phoneticians. A basic understanding of linguistic terminology will go a long way towards helping you in your quest to master the Eyak sound system. Never fear: the sections that follow presume no prior knowledge of linguistics.

Consonants

Simply put, a consonant is a sound that obstructs the flow of air through the vocal tract in some fashion. In the pages that follow, the term “articulator" refers to a feature of the vocal tract that is interacting with another: examples include the tongue, teeth, lips, roof of the mouth, back of the throat, etc. Consonants can be described according to three basic variables, which are detailed below. Any descriptive terms that are not immediately relevant to Eyak phonology, while included for completeness, are presented in gray font.

  • Place of articulation: the location where the vocal tract constricts during the production of the sound. Refer to Figure 2 as you peruse the following list.

    • Bilabial: closure of the two lips, such as in the English letter ‘m’

    • Labiodental: contact between the lips and the teeth

    • Dental: contact between the tip of the tongue and the teeth

    • Alveolar: contact between the tip of the tongue and the alveolum (the roof of the mouth just behind the teeth). As you pronounce the English letter ‘t,’ notice where your tongue touches the roof of the mouth.

    • Postalveolar: contact between the tip of the tongue and the alveolar ridge. If you move your tongue over the roof of your mouth from front to back, you will encounter a bump; this is the alveolar ridge.

    • Retroflex: contact between the tongue tip and the roof of the mouth, somewhere near the alveolar ridge or a little further back, with the tongue slightly curled

    • Palatal: contact between the body of the tongue and the hard palate (the hard stretch of tissue behind the alveolar ridge)

    • Velar: contact between the back of the tongue and the velum. The velum is also known as the soft palate, which is located just behind the hard palate: you’ll be able to feel the change in texture on the roof of your mouth by running your tongue over its surface. Notice where the back of your tongue touches the roof of your mouth when pronouncing the English letter ‘k;’ this is the velum.

    • Uvular: contact between the back of the tongue and the uvula, the flap of tissue that hangs down in the far back of the mouth, near the tonsils

    • Pharyngeal: contact between the back of the tongue and the pharynx, the part of the throat behind the nasal cavity

    • Glottal: closure of the larynx. You can think of this as a sort of “catch" in the back of the throat; we’ll discuss glottal sounds more in the sections that follow.

  • Manner of articulation: the degree to which two articulators make contact, as well as the identity of the articulators in question.

    • Stop: Articulators make contact such that there is a full blockage of airflow through the mouth. The velum (soft palate) is raised so that no air can pass through the nasal passages.

    • Nasal Stop: Articulators make contact such that there is a full blockage of airflow through the mouth; however, the velum (soft palate) is lowered, allowing air to escape the vocal tract through the nose. Prove this to yourself by attempting to say either ‘m’ or ‘n’ while holding your nose.

    • Trill: One articulator makes contact with another and vibrates repeatedly.

    • Tap or Flap: Articulators very briefly make contact, temporarily obstructing airflow through the mouth. Taps/flaps are akin to stops, but the duration of closure is typically shorter and there is minimal pressure build-up.

    • Fricative: Articulators come close to one another but do not make contact. This allows a thin and turbulent stream of air to pass between them, generating a hissing sound.

    • Affricate: a stop followed immediately by a fricative (usually sharing a place of articulation)

    • Approximant: Articulators come close to one another but do not make contact. The resulting opening is not narrow enough to cause audibly turbulent airflow. Approximants are very similar to vowels.

    • Lateral Fricative: The tongue tip makes contact with the roof of the mouth, but air is permitted to flow around the sides of the tongue body. The airstream is constricted, thus causing turbulence.

    • Lateral Approximant: The tongue tip makes contact with the roof of the mouth, but air is permitted to flow around the sides of the tongue body. The airstream is somewhat constricted, but not enough to cause turbulence.

  • Voicing: Sounds are voiced when your vocal cords vibrate and voiceless when they do not. You can hear (and feel!) this distinction by comparing the English consonants ‘d’ and ‘t.’ Try producing each of these sounds with a hand on your throat: you should feel vibrations as you are producing ‘d.’ The sounds are otherwise identical: note that your tongue is in the exact same position (the alveolum) for both.

Consonants are typically described according to the following formula:

voicing + place of articulation + manner of articulation


For example, the English consonant /p/ is produced by closing and then releasing the two lips without engaging the vocal cords: thus, it is voiceless, its place of articulation is bilabial, the manner of articulation is complete oral closure: it is describe as a voiceless bilabial stop. All the consonant sounds a human is capable of producing can be described in this manner.
There are many other features that can be used to describe consonants, and here we will discuss three additional phonetic characteristics that are relevant to the Eyak sound system:

  • Aspiration: Aspirated sounds are accompanied by a puff of air. You can test this for yourself by pronouncing the English words ‘pot’ and ‘spot’ while holding a piece of paper several inches away from your mouth. You should notice that saying the word ‘pot’ causes the paper to move; this is because the ‘p’ in ‘pot’ is aspirated while the ‘p’ in ‘spot’ is unaspirated. Many languages, such as Eyak, consider aspirated and unaspirated sounds to be distinct, whereas aspirated ‘p’ and unaspirated ‘p’ are regarded as the same basic sound in English.

  • Ejectives: Ejectives are sounds accompanied by a constriction of the glottis. Try to produce a “catch" in your throat while simultaneously articulating another sound. If all else fails, try beatboxing: the popping sounds you produce are likely ejectives!

  • Labialization: Labialized sounds are articulated with rounded lips .

Vowels

Vowels are sounds that produced without obstruction of the vocal tract. They are defined by three features, which are listed below. Vowels are typically represented on a trapezoidal chart like the one in Figure [fig:vowelspace]. The shape of the diagram is meant to mimic the shape of the mouth, with the left side representing vowels that are articulated with the tongue further forward (closer to the teeth) and the right side representing vowels that are articulated with the tongue further back (closer to the velum). Most of the vowel sounds available to the world’s languages are depicted on the diagram below. To place these symbols in context, vowels used in the standard American dialect of English are printed in black, whereas other vowels symbols are presented in gray. (Note that the vowel inventory of American English varies by dialect, so your personal collection of vowels may differ!)

  • Frontness:

    • Front: articulated with the tongue further forward in the mouth

    • Central: articulated with the tongue near the center of the mouth

    • Back: articulated with the tongue further back in the mouth

  • Height:

    • High: articulated with the tongue high up in the mouth (Try to feel where your tongue is placed when making the ‘ee’ sound in English, as in “team.")

    • Mid-High: articulated with the tongue high in the mouth, but not quite as high as, say, the ‘ee’ sound.

    • Mid-Low: articulated with the tongue low in the mouth, but not quite as low as, say, the ‘aah’ sound.

    • Low: articulated with the tongue low in the mouth (Try to feel where your tongue is placed when making the ‘aah’ sound in English, like you do when the doctor wants to look at your throat.)

    Roundedness:

    • Rounded: the lips are pursed while articulating the sound

    • Unrounded: the lips are relaxed and open while articulating the sound

Eyak Orthography

Mercifully, the Eyak writing system is much more straightforward than that of English: there is a one-to-one mapping between sounds and letters, with no ambiguity or arcane spelling rules. Most the letters of Eyak are borrowed from the English alphabet, which can give rise to potential confusion, since there are several instances of mismatch: for example, the letter ‘x’ does not correspond to the English [ks], but to a different sound altogether, which may take some time for the Eyak language learner to grow accustomed to. The unfamiliar characters you will encounter include ‘’ and the apostrophe used as a letter. Quite a few sounds in Eyak are represented as digraphs (two letters) or even trigraphs (three letters), and must be treated as a unit. This is not altogether unfamiliar to the English speaker: we write certain sounds with digraphs, such as ‘sh’ and ‘ch.’ Trigraphs, however, are not a part of English standard orthography. In the sections that follow, each sound will be presented with its accompanying written representation.
Early in your studies of Eyak, getting used to the orthography might present something of a challenge, despite the relative consistency of the mapping between letters and sounds. This is because digraphs and trigraphs can contain smaller ‘letter units’ within them; only by reading a bit further ahead will you be able to properly parse a sequence of letters. For example, consider the word “GALAtsitl’ " (land otter). As your eyes move from left to right, you might be tempted to interpret the last three letters as a ‘t’ followed by an ‘l’ followed by a glottal stop. However, pronouncing an individual ‘t’ here would be a mistake; if you read just a bit further, you’ll noticed that this particular combination of letters constitutes a viable trigraph (the ejective voiceless alveolar lateral fricative; see Table 4), and therefore the sounds must be be grouped together. You should approach reading Eyak algorithmically: first scan the word for trigraphs, next scan for digraphs, and whatever letters remain must be interpreted as monographs. As you gain familiarity with the inventory of possible letters, this procedure will become second nature.
I’m sorry to say that I fibbed a little bit above: there is at least some ambiguity in the writing system, arising from different historical conventions. Michael Krauss favored he use of the digraph ‘dj’ in his writings, whereas newer materials will present this sound with just a ‘j.’ Moreover, older texts will use a colon to represent lengthened vowels, as in ‘a:", whereas newer materials will use a double vowel, as in ‘aa.’ The contents of this volume use the newer conventions; however, you should be familiar with both writing systems, in case you choose to peruse archival texts.

The Sounds of Eyak

Now that you are officially a phonetician (having read Section 1!), we will examine Eyak sounds using formal linguistic terminology, alongside more layperson-friendly notes and tips. The Eyak language has 34 consonants and 33 vowels, each of which is described in detail below and accompanied by an audio clip. Each subsection below corresponds to a different manner of articulation; within each subsection, sounds are ordered by place of articulation from the front of the mouth to the back (refer back to Figure 2 for a recap of the possible places of articulation).

Oral stops (also known as plosives) involve a closure in both the mouth and the nasal passages, followed by a release of the closure to permit airflow.1

Vowels

The basic Eyak vowel inventory is rather small, as world languages go. There are five “basic" (or modal) vowels which can be modified in a number of ways, as discussed below. In Figure [fig:eyakvowelspace] below, you will see these five vowels superimposed on the vowel space diagram you saw in Figure [fig:vowelspace]. Notice that there are far fewer basic vowels in Eyak than there are in English.
There are no diphthongs in the Eyak language. Diphthongs occur when two vowels are combined into a single syllable, and they are very common in many dialects of English. For example, in standard American English, the word “out" - while considered monosyllabic - actually contains two vowels: the low front unrounded ‘a’ sound (as in “hat"), which morphs into the high back rounded ‘u’ sound (as in “boot"). In Eyak, however, vowels must be pure.

Not too bad, right? Alas, each of these basic vowels is subject to certain modifications (which you may occasionally see referred to as “stigmata" in certain Eyak grammar texts). The five possible “flavors" of vowel, if you will, are as follows:

  • Glottal: Not creaky voice

  • Aspirated: These vowels are pronounced with an accompanying puff of air. This has the effect of making the vowel sound somewhat “breathy."

  • Long: In some languages, such as English, vowels can be shortened or lengthened based on factors like the speed of speech, emphasis, etc; however, the meaning of a word does not change because the vowel length changes. In Eyak, however (as in many other world languages), changing the length of the vowel changes the word’s meaning entirely: for example, the Eyak word “da" means “sit" and “daa" means “we."2

  • Nasalized: Vowels that are produced with the velum lowered so that some air escapes through the nose. In less technical terms, think of how you would imitate Sponge Bob, or perhaps someone with a head cold. If you really pay attention to what’s going on inside your mouth, you’ll notice something shifting way in the back: that’s your velum lowering, allowing air to flow both through your nose and mouth (refer again to Figure  1).

At this point it probably won’t surprise you to learn that these features can occur in a large number of combinations, as detailed in Tables 7-10 below and summarized in Table 11. Note that the mid central vowel, represented by the letter ‘A’ in Eyak and referred to by linguists as a schwa, does not come in multiple flavors: it is available only in modal, or unmodified, form, the equivalent of vanilla. By the same token, notice that the vowel ‘e’ does not permit all possible variants (see Table 9), whereas the remaining vowels each have nine possible variants. Moreover, ‘e’ can never appear without a modification of some sort.
